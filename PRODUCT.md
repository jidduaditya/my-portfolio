# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Primary: co-founders and product leaders evaluating Aditya as a potential collaborator, partner, or hire. They're scanning for signal: does this person think deeply about product, or just manage backlogs? Secondary: hiring managers and HR screening for senior product roles. They want proof of impact and breadth fast. All visitors arrive with limited attention and high pattern-matching instincts. The site must break through "another PM portfolio" in seconds.

## Product Purpose

A personal portfolio that functions as a proof-of-work artifact. It exists to demonstrate the same craft Aditya brings to product work: clear thinking, considered design, and an ability to ship end-to-end. Success looks like a visitor spending 3+ minutes exploring, remembering the site after closing the tab, and reaching out.

## Brand Personality

Creative, deep, approachable. The voice is a senior practitioner who doesn't take themselves too seriously but takes the work very seriously. Warm without being casual. Confident without being flashy. The kind of person you'd want building your product: someone who sweats the details but keeps the big picture sharp.

## Anti-references

- Generic Wix/Squarespace template portfolios (cookie-cutter layouts, stock patterns, no personality)
- Corporate enterprise slide decks turned into websites (blue gradients, meaningless metrics front-and-center, buzzword density)
- Overly flashy agency sites (parallax everything, animations that delay content, style over substance)
- "AI made this" aesthetic (identical card grids, hero-metric templates, gradient text, glassmorphism)

## Design Principles

1. **Show the craft, don't claim it.** Every design decision is itself a portfolio piece. The site's quality should make the case before any text does.
2. **Depth on demand.** Surface layer is scannable and fast. Deeper layers reward curiosity with real substance (case studies, PRDs, detailed impact).
3. **Warm confidence.** Premium feel without coldness. Generous whitespace and considered typography say "senior" more than any title can.
4. **Memorable, not loud.** One or two distinctive choices that stick in memory, not a wall of effects competing for attention.
5. **Respect the visitor's time.** No friction that doesn't earn its place. Every interaction should feel worth it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline. Sufficient color contrast ratios (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text). Reduced motion support via prefers-reduced-motion. Keyboard-navigable modals and interactive elements. Semantic HTML structure.
